SUMMARY

·        This report provides the first results of a quantitative study undertaken based on an exhaustive compilation of WTO agriculture negotiations related documents since the initiation of the analysis and exchange of information process in 1997.

·        Around 1200 documents were identified and compiled in an excel database, which will be regularly updated.

·        The period covered by the study (1997-2023), was divided into four main phases of similar duration reflecting the evolution of the negotiations: pre-Doha (1997-2001), Doha (2002-2008), post Single undertaking (2009-2015) and ongoing period (2016-2023).

·        This study confirms quantitatively some trends and patterns during the negotiation, such as:

o   The acceleration of activity in advance of the Ministerial Conferences but with some variation over time.

o   The evolution in terms of Members' priorities, notably from market access towards domestic support.

o   The evolution of Members' activity and priorities, as well as the evolution of the role of groups over time.

·        Using networks analysis, the study also provides a representation of the evolution over time of the linkages between various Members and groups of Members, as well as between documents.

·        The next step for this project, subject to available staff resources, would be to undertake in-depth textual analysis of this set of documents using new AI supported text analysis tools including terms' frequency and correlation over time as well as similarity between documents by the same proponents and between different proponents.[1]

1  Introduction

1.1.  This research project was initiated by AGCD and ERSD[2] in 2020 with three main goals:

·      Establish an exhaustive compilation of all the documents related to WTO agriculture negotiations since it started with the initiation of the analysis and exchange of information process in 1997.

·      Develop an analytical method and a tool to undertake quantitative analysis of these documents, by nature, topic, sponsor and content.

·      Undertake textual analysis of the documents.

1.2.  As of August 2023, the resulting outputs of the first phase of the project are:

·      An exhaustive internal database of negotiations related documents since 1997 regularly updated by AGCD[3] and maintained by ERSD.

·      An analytical webtool providing a convenient way to access these documents and data visualizations contained in this report.

1.3.  The question of whether some of these outputs could be made available at a later stage to the Members is not discussed here. On the one hand, the documents compiled were made available to all Members when circulated and the database could present some value for Members willing to engage in historical research. On the other hand, some Members may express sensitivities, including with some technical choices adopted in this project (such as the choice of keywords for the topics).

1.4.  An option could be to make available a simplified version of the tool or just the list of documents. The issue of accessibility of these documents and analytical webtool to a larger audience, especially researchers and agriculture specialists, in conformity with the WTO information derestriction policy could also be raised at a later stage.

1.5.  This report describes the process followed to collect the documents and the resulting output (part 2), the nature of the analytical webtool developed to analyse these data (part 3) and presents some preliminary results, illustrating the kind of work that can be undertaken with this analytical tool (part 4) before concluding on possible next steps (part 5).

2  documents collection

2.1.  The first phase of the project consisted in identifying, listing and compiling the relevant agricultural negotiation-related documents. While the Special Session of the Committee on Agriculture (CoA-SS) to conduct the negotiations for continuing the reform process under Article 20 of the Agreement on Agriculture (AoA) was established in March 2000[4], this compilation also includes the documents circulated in the context of the preparatory analysis and exchange information process initiated at the end of 1996. Indeed, there is an obvious continuity between this phase and the negotiations stricto-sensu.[5]

2.2.  The process of identification and listing of relevant documents was based on pre-existing partial lists available in AGCD and covering various periods of the negotiations, complemented by additional research.

2.3.  The following rules were applied when collecting the documents:

·      The list includes only documents identified with an "agriculture" WTO symbol[6] with the exception of some documents circulated by the Chair of the CoA-SS in 2007-2008.[7] As a result, the list does not include Room Documents or PowerPoint presentations discussed during CoA-SS meetings and circulated to Members without any WTO symbol.[8]

·      The list includes all documents circulated by Members, the Chair or the Secretariat except the reports of dedicated discussions of the relevant trade-related developments for Cotton.[9]

·      The list does not differentiate revisions, addenda or corrigenda that do not change the nature of the document.[10]

·      The list does not include the Ministerial Decisions adopted by Members.

 

2.4.  The almost 1,200 documents[11] resulting from this identification process are listed in an excel spreadsheet constituting the internal database of negotiations related documents and classified using the following criteria:

·      Date and year of circulation;

·      Type of document, i.e. document emanating from (i) Members, (ii) Chair, or (iii) Secretariat;

·      Title;

·      Authors, namely Secretariat, Chair and Sponsors/Proponents for Members' documents[12];

·      Pillar (also named category in this report) covered, based on the title of the document. The category "Export restrictions"[13] was added to the three pillars traditionally identified in the AoA, namely Domestic Support, Market Access and Export Competition. Documents addressing Public Stockholding and the Special Safeguard Mechanism were classified respectively under Domestic Support and Market Access.[14] Documents of general nature that could not be associated with one or more pillars are identified in a residual "X" category;

·      Main topics covered, based on an arbitrary list defined by the Secretariat (see Annex 2). In absence of an identified pillar (category X), the documents are attributed to a residual "X" topic.[15]

 

2.5.  In the last phase of the collection process, the content of all the documents (English version) was compiled in a dataset to be used as the basis for the semantic functionalities of the analytical tool.[16]

2.6.  One difficulty arose from the fact that some of the documents[17] were not available on WTO Documents Online (DOL). While most of the documents were stored by AGCD, it was necessary to retrieve the missing documents in archives or translation sections.[18]

2.7.  All the documents listed in the internal database of negotiations related documents and not available on DOL are now available in AGCD.

3  documents analysis tools

3.1.  This collection of documents provides a rich source of data and a robust dataset for identifying patterns in submissions and the production of proposals and documents over more than 25 years of negotiation history. The subsequent sections aim to explore the data using statistical and text analytical tools.

3.2.  Most analyses were conducted using Python and associated libraries, including pandas, spaCy, NLTK, and NetworkX. Additionally, we developed a webtool that allows users to browse the documents in both their original form and in a processed, analysis-friendly format, enabling interactive examination of the analysis results outlined below.

3.3.  The preliminary version of the tool can be accessed at http://agdocs.herokuapp.com/. It currently provides functions such as browsing document texts, providing statistics on document collection, comparing similarity of documents and generating word cloud.

4  preliminary findings

4.1  Evolution number of documents by year, Member and category

4.1.  This section presents summary statistics in terms of simple counts of documents by year, Member, pillar (or category) and topics. These figures should be considered with a certain caution in light of some methodological limitations regarding:

·      The nature of documents: Although we tried to minimize duplications by removing documents with marginal revision, the documents included in the database are of different nature, including topic-by-topic submissions, analytical papers, statements, declarations, or specific questions to other Members etc.

·      The classification of documents: In some instances (e.g. statements), the link between a document and a category (and/or a topic covered) is not obvious and required a recourse to the X category.

·      The sponsoring Members: Compositions of Member groups referred to as "author" can vary over the years with Members joining or leaving some groups, and several submissions introduced by a subset of group's Members are considered as a general rule as "joint submissions" (see below).

4.2.  To link the statistics with the negotiation timeline, we also presented data according to four different negotiation periods of relatively similar duration identified as the "main negotiation" periods (Figure 1, right):

·      1997-2001: Before the launch of the Doha round (pre-Doha – 6 years),

·      2002-2008: Doha round until December 2008 draft modalities (Doha 7 years),

·      2009-2015: Post December 2008 "depression" period followed by the end of the "single undertaking" in 2011 and the follow up successful MC9 (Bali) and MC10 (Nairobi) Ministerial Conferences called here post single undertaking (PSU – 7 years),

·      2016-August 2023: Post Nairobi or ongoing period (OGP – 8 years).[19]

4.3.  The numbers of documents vary considerably across years, ranging from 4 in 2012 to a maximum of 195 in 2001. Unsurprisingly, the pattern broadly relates to important events that happened along with about 25 years of negotiation history (Figure 1). Four peak years, 2001, 2006, 2015 and 2017, correspond to the preparation of the launch of the Doha negotiation, the post Cancun negotiation intensification period in 2005-06 (though the continuation of this sequence until July 2008 was marked by a reduction in the number of documents) and the intensive negotiations leading to the 2015 Nairobi and 2017 Buenos Aires Ministerial Conferences. By contrast, the preparation of the 2013 Bali Ministerial Conference was characterized by a low number of submissions.

4.4.  About 70% of the 1,197 documents were issued between 1997 and 2008, while the rest were published after 2009. In the pre-Doha period, Members explored different topics with a focus on various technical issues. Among the 405 documents, 86% were Members' submissions, 12% were Secretariat's technical papers, with the remaining 2% being Chair's documents (Figure 1). Those documents prepared the foundation for intensive negotiations as part of the Doha Development Agenda.


 

4.5.  During 2002-2008, the critical period of the DDA, Members submitted 273 documents to describe, clarify and recalibrate their negotiation positions. It is also during this period that the Chair and the Secretariat were most active in producing documents to facilitate and support the negotiations, with 66 Chair's documents (including reports) and 71 Secretariat papers being issued.

4.6.  The number of documents declined significantly after 2009 as the Doha Round entered into deadlock and increased again after 2015. Only 146 and 236 documents were published during the periods 2009-2015 and 2016-2023 (102 and 181 documents, respectively, by Members). Although the share of Chair documents still accounted for close to 20% of these documents, Secretariat papers have reduced to 12% and 2%, respectively, in the last two periods.

Figure 1: Number of documents by year/period and author

 

4.7.  Member proposals issued under different pillars varied during the successive periods (Figure 2). During the pre-Doha period, about the same numbers of proposals were submitted under market access (MA), domestic support (DS) and export competition (EC). 50% of documents circulated during this pre-Doha period were not pillar-specific (X), with a peak in 2000 and 2001 before the launch of the Doha round. This is partially explained by the general nature of the contributions during this period before entering into more structured negotiations periods as of 2002 characterized by more topic specific submissions. Another related technical explanation stems from the fact that Members' interventions during meetings circulated as documents during this period, as well as formal CoA meetings in special session reports[20], were classified here under the "X" category.

4.8.  During the Doha negotiations period (2002-2008), more submissions focused on MA (46%) than on the other two pillars. The number of documents under the three pillars was rebalanced during the period 2009-2015 (34% for MA, 23% for DS and 19% for EC). The share of export competition related submissions among the 3 pillars peaked in 2015, but significantly decreased after the adoption of the December 2015 Nairobi decision on export competition.

4.9.  Domestic Support overtook Market Access as the main pillar in terms of submissions during the period 2016-2023. This latter trend confirms the progressive shift of priority by Members:

·      On the one hand, less interest for market access opening in the multilateral setting not only in agriculture, but also for industrial products and services to the benefit of regional negotiations.

·      On the other hand, a remaining and even increasing interest for domestic support, as this pillar, including PSH, can only be negotiated in a multilateral setting to avoid free-riding and remains a source of concern for most Members, either to level the playing field or to address increasing applied support by some Members.

4.10.  Finally, export restrictions (ER) attracted more interest as of 2017, but still less than domestic support and market access.

Figure 2: Number of Members' documents by year/period and pillar

4.11.  The number of Members' submissions, including timing and pillars, also provide some useful indication on their activity and priorities in different periods of negotiations.

4.12.  Figure 3 provides the list of the top 20 Members or groups in terms of number of submissions disaggregated by period and pillar. It confirms the reduced activity for most authors during the period 2009-2015 with the noticeable exception of the two groups represented in the top 20, namely the G-33 (which started being active in 2004) and the Cairns Group.

4.13.  Regarding Members' contributions, some Members, either developed such as the United States, Switzerland, Australia or Canada, or developing such as Argentina or Pakistan, have consistently maintained a certain level of activity across all periods, from moderate to intense.

4.14.  The figure also confirms the clear intensification of the contributions by Cairns Group members (Australia, Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, Costa Rica, Peru…) in the last period 2018-2022, while the number of contributions by the Cairns Group as a whole significantly decreased, as they were gradually replaced by contributions of "sub-groups" of Cairns Group members.

4.15.  Some Members like the EU, Norway, Cuba and Dominican Republic, are characterized on the other hand by a progressive reduction of their number of submissions and low level in the last period and complete inactivity in the case of Cuba[21], that may be interpreted by a shift in their engagement level or strategic focus, and/or some degree of fatalism.

4.16.  When it comes to subject matter, or "pillars", the figure also confirms the clear change in priorities noted above towards domestic support. This is particularly the case for Cairns Group members as well as for the G-33 (mainly on PSH). On the other hand, G-10 members, including Switzerland, Japan, Korea, and Chinese Taipei, have been leaning more towards export restrictions in the last period. The figure also indicates some specific patterns such as the focus on market access with offensive interests by the United States, Argentina, Costa Rica, Paraguay and Uruguay during the period 2002-2008 (with tropical products being a specific topic of interest for some of them). These Members are again active on market access in the 2016-2022 period (plus Brazil and Australia). The G-33 has also been active on market access during the Doha round period but from a defensive point of view (special products and SSM).

4.17.  These varying focuses not only reveal individual Member priorities but also shape the collective direction of negotiations.

4.18.  The number of submissions by topic (Figure 4) shows interesting patterns. These patterns go beyond mere categorization by pillar. The volume of documents associated with each topic varies, ranging from submissions focused solely on a single topic to those that discuss a wide array of topics across one or even all of the pillars.

4.19.  Overall, special and differential treatment[23] is unsurprisingly the most frequently included topic in the submissions, surpassing non-trade concerns but also food security (Figure 4).

4.20.  On market access, submissions have focused more on tariff rate quotas and tariff reductions, followed by SSM and SSG.[24] This seems to reflect rather well the degree of priority given by proponents to the central component of market access represented by tariffs, and to TRQs until 2013 and the adoption of the 2013 Bali Decision, before the sensitive issue of safeguards. The next topics of interest are special products, tariff simplification and tariff escalation, sensitive products (during the 2004-2008 period), and the couple preference erosion and tropical products albeit with slightly less submissions for the latter.

4.21.  On export competition, the number of submissions addressing export subsidies is higher than for export finance and food aid, with exporting STEs being last. This illustrates the fact that Members have generally addressed the four issues in parallel, though with some priority granted to export subsidies.

4.22.  On Domestic support, the Amber Box is by far the topic most frequently addressed, followed by Blue and Green boxes with almost the same number of submissions each. This hierarchy can be seen as confirming Members' priorities being proportional to the distortion effect of the different boxes. The last group for domestic support is composed of the OTDS and more surprisingly the development box. It should be noted here that the classification by topic is somehow less straight forward for domestic support, than for the other pillars.

4.23.  Cotton and export restrictions are in the middle range of submissions.

4.24.  Finally, the number of submissions with reference to specific groups of Members is very close for SVEs, LDCs and NFIDCs, with RAMs lagging behind.

Figure 4: Number of submissions by topic

 

4.25.  The change of pattern in different topics over time confirms that Members' priorities have evolved over time. This is notably the case for SSM, which has been addressed by more than twice as many submissions as PSH, despite the centrality acquired by this latter topic in the negotiations over the last 10 years. This evolution is illustrated by the charts in Figure 5 which illustrate the fact that SSM has been present in the negotiations since 2001, with peaks of interest in 2001-02, 2006, 2010, 2015 and 2017, while PSH became a topic of interest after 2013, in 2015, 2017 and more recently 2021 and 2022. Finally, special products (the last topic of interest for the G-33) was addressed mainly in 2006 and 2007, while Members were negotiating a tariff reduction formula.

Figure 5: Evolution of submission on specific topics

 

4.26.  Groups of Members have been playing an important role in the negotiations, and data indicate that the role of groups in the submission of documents has evolved over time during each of the four periods identified above.

4.27.  We calculated the number of documents submitted by single Members, by groups of Members, or jointly by some Members in an ad-hoc manner, i.e., not in constituted groups (Figure 6). In the pre-Doha period (1997-2001), most documents were submitted by single Members (84%). This could be explained by the fact that Members started formulating their own initial positions during this period, while most groups had not yet reactivated/formed to pursue a coordinated approach. This is also explained by the nature of the Analysis and Information Exchange and pre-Doha negotiation processes, in which Members were asked to express their views individually and individual statements during meetings were recorded as written documents.

4.28.  In the Doha period (2002-2008), more than half of the documents (53%) were produced by groups or jointly by Members. This may illustrate the willingness of smaller Members to give more weight to their views by allying with other Members.

4.29.  Between 2009-2015, more than half (58%) of documents were submitted by groups, while only 11% were joint proposals and less than a third (31%) were signatured by single Members. This can be explained by the low number of submissions during this period, corresponding partly to the post 2008 "depression" inactive period and partly to the preparation of the 2013 Ministerial Conference that quickly focused on a small number of well-identified topics through a Chair-led process.

4.30.  In the last period, Members have been working mainly individually or with other Members (around 75%), with Group documents declining to around a quarter of the total number of documents only. This may be explained by a Groups' weakening trend: First, within the Cairns Group (see above) with submissions being more and more sponsored by only a "group of Members" of the Group, classified here as joint submissions[25]; second, the de facto disappearance of the G-20[26] and a reduced activity of the G-10 as a Group, as positions in the Group are less consensual on domestic support and export restrictions than on market access.

Figure 6: Share of documents by a single Member, by group or as a joint document

 

4.31.  Figure 7 shows a clear seasonal pattern in the issuance of documents, unsurprisingly characterized by much fewer documents being released in January and August, which are the traditional vacation seasons for the diplomats. Conversely, the number of documents reaches its peaks in June when intensive negotiations occur before the summer break and in November, which usually corresponds to the final stretch before most Ministerial Conferences, held at the end of the year.

4.2  Networks

4.32.  The large collection of documents provides an opportunity to explore the relationship between various parameters in the negotiation history. In this section, network analysis is used to explore two kinds of relationships: (1) between Members and (2) between documents.

4.33.  Network analysis is a field of study that deals with the analysis of complex systems, such as social networks, computer networks, and biological networks. It is a powerful tool for understanding the relationships between entities, whether they be people, countries, or any other type of object. In network analysis, relationships are represented by nodes (also called vertices) and edges (also called links or connections) in a graph. A node represents an entity, while an edge represents a relationship between two entities. Importantly, a node without connection will not be represented in the chart.

 

4.34.  By using visualization tools to generate graphs and applying network analysis techniques to interpret them, hidden patterns and relationships that may not be immediately apparent can be uncovered. This could be useful for exploring complex systems such as negotiation history, where the relationships between entities can be difficult to discern without the aid of network analysis.

4.35.  The following graphs are generated using NetworkX - a Python library dedicated to the creation, manipulation, and analysis of intricate networks and graphs - multigraphs designed to accommodate multiple edges connecting pairs of nodes.

4.2.2  Networks by authoring/sponsoring Members

4.36.  In the context of negotiation documents, network analysis can be used to explore the connections between Members based on their joint authorship of documents. In a network of authoring Members, nodes represent Members, and edges represent the fact that Members have jointly published a document, which indicates that the Members share the same or similar views on the issue that the document addresses.

4.37.  Through an analysis of co-publication frequencies, valuable insights can be gleaned concerning shared perspectives and collaborative tendencies among Members. Notably, as the quantity of shared documents between nodes increases, the proximity between them is reduced.

4.38.  It was chosen here to identify a group's contribution as one contribution (one dot) rather than a network of dots composed of all the groups members, to avoid over-representing "intra-group" activities (and making the graphs less intelligible). It is also important to keep in mind that Members or groups of Members not having co-authored documents will not appear on the graph, which should therefore be interpreted only as a visualisation of joint activity.

4.39.  The four network graphs are corresponding to the four periods identified as the "main negotiation" periods (see above):

o  1997-2001: pre-Doha period,

o  2002-2008: Doha period,

o  2009-2015: Post single undertaking period (PSU),

o  2016-end of 2022: ongoing period (OGP).

 

4.40.  During the first "pre-Doha" period until November 2001 (Figure 8.1), four main communities of Members can be identified:

•   A first one mainly composed of G-10 and other European members (EU and future EU member States) generally defensive on market access;

•   A second one composed of ACP countries, mainly from the Caribbean islands, close to the first community as a result of some joint submissions[27];

•   A third community of developing countries from Africa, Asia and Latin America defensive on market access (constituting the basis of the future G-33) more distant from the European group, with Egypt somehow in the middle between the second and third groups;

•   A fourth community composed of "offensive" exporting developing countries mainly from South America, plus Thailand and Malaysia, all members of the Cairns Group.[28]

4.41.  The Philippines and Indonesia, which are part of both the Cairns Group and the future G-33 are placed in the third group at the border with the fourth one. More surprisingly, this is also the case for India, due to a joint submission on export finance with more offensive Members.

4.42.  The United States who only made individual submissions, as well as Australia, Canada and New Zealand, the three developed Cairns Group members who contributed either alone or as part of the Cairns Group (also missing in the absence of joint submissions with other Members or groups of Members) are absent from the chart. Would they be represented on the chart, these Members would obviously be part of the fourth family.

Figure 8.1: 1997-2001 - Before the launch of the Doha round (pre-Doha)

 

4.43.  In the "Doha" period representation between 2002 and 2008 (Figure 8.2), the following patterns can be visualized:

·      Members are generally closer to each other as a result of some joint submissions between Members from different communities identified above.

·      The G-10 members are inter-related to one another due to several joint submissions between a subset of Members and are closer to the large group of "defensive" developing countries due to some joint submissions by its developing country Members, namely Korea and Mauritius.

·      The EU is getting more distant from the G-10 and future EU member States, the latter getting closer to other recently acceded Members such as Jordan, Georgia or Moldova. The EU gets in parallel closer to the US, notably with the game-changing pre-Cancun August 2003 joint submission. The EU also appears to be closer to Canada, New Zealand, Australia and a few other Cairns Group members, as well as Egypt, following several joint submissions on various simulations.

·      China appears in the game, in a rather central position relatively close to India and Brazil, who is also positioned in a more central role as both leader of the G-20, created in August 2003 and member of the Cairns Group.

·      Regarding groups, the Cairns Group and the G-20 are linked as a result of their joint submission on export subsidies elimination timeline, while the G-33, who got active in 2004, the ACP Group, the African Group and the LDC Group are connected by their joint communication on special products and SSM. Other groups such as the G-10, the C-4 or the "group of Article XII" members did not submit joint submissions with other Members.

4.44.  The third "post single undertaking" period (Figure 8.3) shows a very different picture, with less links due to a reduced number of submissions (see section 4.1 above) and more limited signs of joint activity.

4.45.  The EU appears to be closer to some Cairns Group members, as a result of a joint submission on export competition in advance of MC10, while subsets of Cairns Group members continue to introduce joint submissions; subsets of G-10 members continue to cosponsor submissions on export restrictions without Norway; several key Members such as the US, China or India are not visible in the absence of joint submissions on their side.

4.46.  In terms of groups, the SVEs, G-33, ACP and African Groups are linked by a joint submission on the SSM, while the NFIDC Group cosponsors in 2011 two submissions on the WTO response to the food crisis with the Arab Group and the African Group following the food prices spikes of 2008 and 2010. The G-90, regrouping the LDC, African and ACP Groups does not appear on the chart despite its two submissions with a strong component on agriculture, as they are identified as "one group" submission.

4.47.  The landscape again changes in the fourth and ongoing "Post Nairobi" period (Figure 8.4). It shows three distinct regions of collaboration:

·      First, a large group of Members from various communities, including the EU, the US, some Cairns Group, G-10 and G-33 members. Rather than illustrating some drastic changes, in the alliances, this cloud results from various joint submissions outside the usual grouping boundaries in particular the Singapore-led proposal of the Decision by the General Council in December 2020 including many cosponsors from different constituencies, but also the EU-US-Japan-Canada July 2021 paper on transparency, joint papers between Canada and G-10 members on export competition, or a submission by the US, Kenya and Uganda in December 2017. ·      Finally, a link between India and China reflecting the 2017 joint submission on domestic support.

4.2.3  Networks by documents

4.49.  Although a negotiation document exists as an independent piece of texts transmitting specific information, proposals or views, it can relate to other documents by referring to their symbols. Such links can be of different nature, either as part of a list of documents, to comment on it or to place the new document in the continuity of the referred one.

4.50.  To analysis the relationship between the documents, we built a document network through the symbols contained in documents. We firstly extracted all documents symbols with text extraction rules from each document. Then a directed graph was built with symbols of documents that refer to other documents as source nodes and documents being referred as target nodes.

4.51.  The graph illustrating the relationship between documents can be found in Figure 9. The nodes in red are documents that only refer to one or more other documents, while nodes in green are documents that are only referred to by other documents. The nodes in blue are documents which refer to other documents and at the same time, also being referred to by others. The size of the node represents the number of documents related to it, both referring or being referred.

4.52.  Figure 9 is complemented by Table 1 which lists the top 20 documents with most references to other documents and the top 20 ones that are most referred to. This latter list includes symbols of ministerial documents that are not included in the compilation.

4.53.  Without surprise the top "referred to" document is by far the December 2008 draft Chair's modalities TN/AG/W/4/Rev.4. It is followed by the 1995 CoA decision on notifications formats G/AG/2. A couple of other Chair's draft modalities are also included in the top 20, as well as several key decisions such as the August 2004 framework package (WT/L/579), the Hong Kong Ministerial Decision (WT/MIN(05)/DEC, and last but not least the 2013 Bali Decision on PSH (WT/MIN(13)/38), and related 2014 General Council decision (WT/L/939). Several Members' submissions, such as a proposal by a group of developing countries on the Green Box or a proposal by the Cairns Group on market access are also in the list.

 

4.54.  The top 20 list of documents with most references should be interpreted with some caution as few documents mainly compose of lists or tables with references to previous documents, in particular those by the Secretariat and were shortened for this exercise as these lists/tables were considered as non-informative documents. With this in mind, it appears that the document with most references is the recent proposal by Costa Rica on domestic support (JOB/AG/243) composed of a first explanatory part with many references to past documents. Other draft decisions, by Chairs such as the draft decision by the CoA-SS Chair in JOB/AG/232, or by Members such as the draft decision on transparency in JOB/AG/213 or several draft decisions on SSM and PSH, also rank high. Remaining documents are mainly composed of Members' analytical communications referring to other documents/past decisions.

4.3  Size of documents

4.55.  Using this compilation of documents, we have created a text corpus that will allow us to explore the subtle details of the language within the texts. A thorough examination requires more effort and will be included in future analyses (see section 5 - conclusion). However, we will present in this section a preliminary set of indicators that employ text-level statistics to measure the document's size.

4.56.  The collection of 1,197 documents demonstrates a wide range of sizes.[29] Document lengths span from hundreds of words to more than 40,000 words, with an average of 2,100 words. Figure 10 provides insight into the top 20 documents with the highest word count, revealing that the longest document - exceeding 40,000 words[30] is a 105 pages 2003 meeting report incorporating verbatim statements by Members.

4.57.  Among the 30 largest documents, 14 are reports of meetings from the Secretariat and 11 are Chair's papers, mostly draft modalities circulated between 2006 and 2008. The only submission coming from Members contains the proceeding (presented papers) of a Conference held in Norway in 2000 on non-trade concerns (document G/AG/NG/W/36 and its revision). Other documents are three documents by the Secretariat including the 2021 compilation of facilitators reports in JOB/AG/201.

4.58.  Generally, the count of sentences aligns well with the word count. This is however less the case for the count of paragraphs.

Figure 10: Size of documents - number of words, sentences, and paragraphs

4.59.  Figure 11 adds further depth to our analysis by exploring the text sizes from various perspectives. In the top-left panel, it confirms that the periods 2000-2003 and 2005-2008 witnessed more extensive activity and text production, as already demonstrated in Figure 1 on the number of documents per year. The figure also confirms that the period 2009-2014, and particularly 2012 and 2014, have registered the lowest output. The fact that text production in 2005-2006 was significantly higher than in 2015 and 2017, despite a rather similar number of submissions, can be explained by the higher proportion of Secretariat's documents, including reports of bigger size during the period 2005-2006, as illustrated by the average number of words per document in these two periods. The peak in number of words per document in 2011 may be the result of one very long Chair's report (TN/AG/26) out of a very small number of documents, while the one in 2021 can also be interpreted by the high share of Chair's reports in the documents issued that year.

4.60.  Among the four pillars, market access followed by domestic support stand out for generating the most substantial textual content, surpassing export competition and export restriction. The residual "X" category outpaces the four topics. This ranking is the same as in the case of number of documents by topic, but with a higher average number of words by document for the residual category (around 3,000 words/document), then for the 3 traditional pillars (around 1,500 words/document) followed by export restrictions (around 800 words/document).

4.61.  In terms of topics, "special and differential treatment (SDT)" which is the most frequently included topic in the submissions (see section 4 above) also commands the largest textual footprint. More broadly the 10 topics with the largest textual footprint are part of the first 11 topics in terms of related documents, with the exception of cotton which ranks 13th for the count by number of documents and 4th for the word count, as a result of a higher average number of words by document (around 2,400 words/document) than the other topics (average varying between 1,800 and 1,400 words/document). Notably, the major contributors of these texts are the EU, Japan, Norway, Mauritius, and the United States. It is worth mentioning that while some of these contributors might not have the highest document count, they have made significant textual contributions due to a high average number of words by document. This is notably the case for Norway, Mauritius, Korea and to a lesser extent Switzerland, all members of the G-10, which may be partially explained by their cosponsoring of the proceedings of the Conference held in Norway on non-trade concerns (document G/AG/NG/W/36 and its revision). By contrast, Japan which ranks first and is also a member of the G-10 has a lower average number of words by document, which may notably result from its numerous short individual submissions.

5  Conclusion and way forward

5.1.  The initial results of this project provide useful quantitative information that inform the existence of various trends and changes during the more than 25 years history of the negotiations.

5.2.  However, this project also confirms that such quantitative analysis must be completed by a qualitative assessment, to fully understand the underlying data and trends, for two main reasons:

·      First, the mixed nature of documents included in the database (topic-by-topic submissions, negotiation proposals, analytical papers, statements, declarations, specific questions to other Members …);

·      Second, the fact that submissions have different values in the negotiations: while some may not bring much new, as they largely build upon previous ones[31], a few others may constitute real game changers such as the pre-Cancun August 2003 joint EU-US submission or 2012 G-20 submissions that paved the way towards the MC9 outcomes.

5.3.  Looking forward, our intention would be to:

        (i)   Continue updating the database and related internal tool to keep an exhaustive basis of WTO documents related to the agriculture negotiations since 1997.

       (ii)   Continue improving the database and related tools to facilitate further research and get feedback and suggestions.

 

      (iii)   Subject to available staff resources, undertake in-depth textual analysis in the set of documents including:

o   Terms' frequency analysis to look at the evolution over time of various terms/expressions (e.g., food security) used in all or a subset of submissions (by Members and/or topics). This analysis would help detecting the apparition of new concepts/approaches in the negotiations and better assess the relative importance of issues at various moments in time. [32]

o   Terms' proximity analysis to look at the evolution over time of the proximity of various terms/expressions (e.g., food security and market access) used in all or a subset of submissions.

o   Similarity between documents to evaluate the degree of proximity between different documents from a lexical point of view, notably to assess quantitatively the speed of evolution of documents.
